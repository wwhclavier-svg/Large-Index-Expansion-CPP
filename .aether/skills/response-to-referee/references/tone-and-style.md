# Tone and Style Guidelines

## Core Principles

1. **Professional Respect** — Reviewers volunteer their time. Always begin from respect for their expertise.
2. **Objectivity** — Respond to the content of comments, not their tone. Use evidence and reasoning.
3. **Balance** — Neither servile nor defensive. The goal is scholarly dialogue, not winning an argument.

---

## Expressing Gratitude

### When to Thank

- At the opening of the response document
- When a comment genuinely improves the paper
- When a comment identifies a real error

### Preferred Phrasing

- "We thank the reviewer for this thoughtful comment."
- "We appreciate the reviewer's careful reading."
- "The reviewer raises an important point that has helped us improve the manuscript."

### Avoid

- Excessive or generic praise that seems insincere
- Thanking for every single comment regardless of substance (reserve genuine thanks for substantive points)

---

## Phrasing Disagreements

Follow a four-step framework:

1. **Acknowledge** — Recognize what is reasonable in the comment
2. **State** — Express disagreement directly but politely
3. **Evidence** — Support your position with reasoning, citations, or paper content
4. **Offer** — Where possible, show willingness to address the underlying concern

### Examples

**When the reviewer misunderstands:**

> We thank the reviewer for raising this concern. We believe there may have been a misunderstanding regarding [topic]. In [Section X], we state that [...], which addresses this point. We have clarified the language to make this more explicit.

**When the suggested change would not improve the paper:**

> We appreciate the reviewer's suggestion. After careful consideration, we have decided to maintain our current approach because [reason with evidence]. However, we have added a brief discussion in [Section Y] to provide additional context.

**When the request is out of scope:**

> The reviewer raises an interesting point about [topic]. While we agree this is important, it falls outside the scope of the present work. We have noted it as a direction for future research in the Discussion section.

## Hedging and Certainty

### Use Hedging For

Interpretations, limitations, comparisons with other work, future directions.

| Too Definite | Appropriately Hedged |
|--------------|---------------------|
| This proves | This suggests / provides evidence for |
| It is clear that | Our results indicate / It appears that |
| The reason is | A possible reason is |

### Be Definite For

Descriptions of what was done, direct findings, changes made in revision.

- "We have added..."
- "We modified the equation to..."
- "The data show..."

---

## Language Patterns to Avoid

| Avoid | Alternative |
|-------|-------------|
| "Obviously, the reviewer is wrong" | "We respectfully differ on this point" |
| "We cannot accept this suggestion" | "We have concerns about this approach" |
| "The reviewer failed to understand" | "We may not have expressed this clearly enough" |
| "We already explained this" | "As discussed in Section X, ..." |
| "This is not our job" | "This falls outside our current scope" |
